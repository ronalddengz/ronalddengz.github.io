rough fish

Greg had been waiting in his Dentist’s office since, oh, about eleven thirty. His appointment was not until two, but the lobby always had the newest issues of outdoor fishing magazines like Trout World and Fly Fly Fishing, and he found the receptionist cute. Oh, what was her name again? Sandra? Samantha? He’d come here so many times before, but he had only heard her name once when his Dentist called for her. Their interactions were limited to patient intake forms and tight-lipped smiles — Greg because he was insecure about his teeth (they were fine), and Sally (Sally!) because the Dentist had told her to at least try to appear a little more welcoming to the guests, yes, even the ones who sat in the dimmest corner of the room leafing through bait catalogues. Greg once had a dream where he asked Sally out for coffee on one such intake form, probably by filling in “Yearning, romantic heart” in the “other” option for “pre-existing health conditions.” He had since forgotten this dream and could only remember waking up with sweaty palms once.

Greg learned about a new species of fish that day between cautious glances up at Sally. Sally was slowly tuning out the office’s muzak while leaving a deeper and deeper Sally-shaped impression in her revolving chair. She was trying hard to make it until she could pound mimosas with her good friends from high school as they did every weekday night.

Sally finally summoned Greg into the Dentist’s office. The Dentist’s name Greg remembered even less clearly than the receptionist’s because he did not find the Dentist cute, and the first time the Dentist had introduced himself to Greg, Greg had been busy fantasizing about ice fishing with Sally off Anchorage.

On the Dentist’s chair, Greg pretended to care about the Dentist’s kids and two dogs, but congratulated the Dentist on sending his son off to optometry school anyways, whatever. Greg likes having his teeth cleaned. He is insecure about his teeth even though they are fine. One day in the first grade, the class menace Derek had told him that his front teeth were yellow, and poor young Gregory had taken it to heart. The Dentist finds Greg’s cleaning obsession strange but as long as Greg keeps coming in, the Dentist can keep on sending more of his kids off to optometry schools. When the Dentist turned on the Cavitron, Greg could no longer discern what exactly the Dentist was talking about but nodded anyways when he found the dull vibrations of the Dentist’s voice agreeable. /// FILL IN He looked up at the bright light shining down on him and thought of Sally, sturgeon migration patterns, the nature of god and of man, but mostly Sally. Cleanings were ritualistic at this point. Each cleaning was just as exciting as the last, like holding hands for the first time with someone he respected or bowling a perfect game.
